Queens Herald Church Website Redesign
User Research

Time | April- Aug 2018
Role | Research Lead
Team | Ency Lu, Nick Moy

Overview

In April 2018, a group of 3 UX enthusiasts set out to revamp our church’s website: qhc.org. The website was first launched in 2014. Since then, it hasn’t been kept up to date, with the design, layout, and most of the features looking very archaic and hard to use. We knew the importance of user research before setting out to redesigning the website. However, it was critical to get buy-in from our project owner, church pastor, in order to launch research.

I led the user research segment of the redesign process, and drafted the user research plan detailing our goals and methods. Then as a team, we brainstormed the survey and interview question. After taking turns interviewing users, we conducted affinity mapping to draw out overarching themes and pain points. From the survey results, I analyzed and aggregated the data into visual results and presented them to our church pastor.

Research Plan

Being cognizant that we have attendees from both Chinese and English congregations, with the help from church staff, the surveys were translated in Chinese and launched via Google Surveys. The surveys were shared through church’s Facebook pages. To offer another form of access for those who don’t use Facebook, we also handed out paper surveys at church.

Research Objective:

To understand who our current users are,
which features do they use the most frequently,
and their painpoints.

Survey Results

Persona

Based on the qualitative and quantitative data from interviews and surveys, I created 3 personas to represent the core users of the website. They are users from the Chinese and English congregations, and volunteer staff who is in charge of making website updates.

Data Analysis

Users tell us although they only attend one of our three congregations, they often need to access information that pertains to the others as well. This comes up when a family has members who attend multiple congregations, and need to plan for or around church events across congregations. The current design of the website divorces the three congregations’ information, making it hard to navigate across-congregation.

Having a consolidated location with information from both sides also messages that we are a church that is united and care about what’s happening in the other congregation, despite the different languages and cultures.

Main Issues:

  1. Information is not centralized or updated

  2. Lack of content about who we are and our ministries

  3. Not mobile friendly

Next Steps

Final Outcome

We presented our research findings to our project owner. Having real data from real people was super convincing and helped to narrate a compelling story of the needs and pain points of our users. Then, our project owner and church leadership reviewed our proposal and was on board with our call-to-actions for redesign. The next steps would involve us working with project owner to acquire content for redesign.

Reflection

This project was a tremendous learning experience for me, applying everything I’ve learned in a real life scenario, (writing a user research plan, drafting survey and interview questions, analyzing data) and leading a ux team to spearhead this project. Each component was a challenge. My biggest takeaway was realizing how important communication was, especially in convincing leadership the importance of research (understanding the problem scope first before delving into redesign). In the end, it was rewarding to see that the results of research could impact the church organization and help bring about change for our users.
