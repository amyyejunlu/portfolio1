How can technology support the ways in which people interact with public art?

In collaboration with Metro 21 and the Heinz College, Arts Management program, our team of five in User Centered Research & Evaluation embarked on a research exploration in answering this question - How can technology support the ways in which people interact with public art?

We approached this project through a series of research methods and evaluations, conducting background research, user interviews, storyboard/speeding dating, as well as an experience prototype to test our ideas.

Background Research + Analysis

Before we dived into the project, each person conducted background research on the public art domain to find out more about the problem space. I synthesized the data based on previous groups’ video and resources, and created a stakeholder map and a customer journey map.

Key Insights from Background Research:

  1. Public art installations are open to many interpretations and it can be used in all possible ways.
  2. Without taking into account location and its neighboring environment, public art can fail to execute what it was designed to do.
  3. Temporary installations may be a better medium of choice for public art. It captures the zeitgeist of the time, staying very relatable and relevant. Being a for only a short window of time also makes it especially valuable.

Walk the Wall

Then, as a team, in our first group work session, we conducted a “walk the wall” activity where we post all our previous research along with our insights and findings to the wall, to visually comprehend each other’s findings and come to a common understanding about how people interact with public art.

Initial Project Definition:

How might we personalize interaction with public art, so that it leaves people with a memorable experience?

Interviewing Tourists and Locals

Next, to prepare our interviews, we drafted a contextual inquiry as well as an observe & intercept guide to find out about the needs/motivations of people behind viewing public art.

We recognized that there are two different types of audiences that we might see: tourists and locals of Pittsburgh. This is important to recognize because the places that the individuals we interview come from can affect their memories and perceptions of art. Thus, we crafted two sets of questions in order to take the differences between these two end user groups into consideration.

Affinity Diagramming Our Interview Findings

From our interview findings, we converted them into meaningful interpretation notes and insights, and put compiled into a master excel sheet. From there, we grouped and then clustered them into meaningful areas of issues/ideas/concepts and overall concerns.

Pink post-it notes described the common issues across the blue post-it note groups. Then they were clustered under the green labels, which grouped the categories together.

Top Needs Identified by Affinity Diagramming:

  1. Public art can be used to diversify space and vary the array of possiblities of uses.
  2. Public art can create a sense of belonging. They invite people to their space.
  3. Public art should allow for dynamic interactions that support the interaction between a visitor of one with the art and amongst groups.
  4. People don't remember the details of the art itself, but instead remember the experiences they had because of the people they were with or the events that took place that day.

Experience Prototyping

We landed on a public art piece that invites people to engage with it by standing on one of two foot pads. When one person stands on each pad, something happens. We decided for the purpose of testing the interaction and value, it didn’t much matter what the something that happened was as long as it was obvious.

In order to assess this interaction idea, we constructed a low-fidelity prototype of our own art piece that looked realistic enough for people to partake in this experience. We used cardboard, construction paper, and two lamps to create the ‘light’. We then used yellow tiles with footprints for the inviting interaction.

Our team decided to come together as all 5 members to conduct the experience prototyping, and this ended up being one of the most fun and successful user testing sessions to date. Having enough people to take notes, document, Wizard of Oz, and recruit made it the entire session run smoothly and helped us encounter more participants in a relatively short period of time.

While our Wizard of Oz execution was clunky due to the nature of our prototype, it was also extremely beneficial in allowing us to test out a solution for our need without easy access to power outlets, and allowed us to test out the experience in different settings (i.e. in front of a Museum as well as in front of a bus stop), to provide us for further opportunities to test the solution.

“It’s so cool that it’s a collaboration for two, but a puzzle for one.”

“If I walked up to this and stepped on it and it lit up, that would be pretty dope.”

Poster Design to Communicate Our Research + Idea

In our final deliverable, our team synethesized the findings into a final poster design that persuasively presents our solution to our problem and addresses the user needs identified in our research. My role was designing the poster and creating its graphics.

I used very minimal color in order to contrast the neon lighting in our solution. The black lines symbolized an electrical circuit, and were designed to connect different points in our process that led to the final solution.

30s Elevator Pitch

Citizens of Pittsburgh are not as interested in public art as we would like them to be considering the costs. They become numb to it when it is a part of their daily life, and they are not willing to read long plaques.The Art + Human Circuit is an interactive life-size “circuit” that empowers citizens to connect with public art and each other.

This solution explores how public art can provide people the opportunity to break away from their daily routines, and to share those moments with the people around them.
